# GeoMEOL
Gram-negative bacterial effectors play important roles in host-pathogen interactions and bacterial pathogenesis. However, most existing deep learning methods still rely primarily on sequence information, making it difficult to capture the spatial conformational features closely related to effector function, as well as their relationship with sequence semantics. To address this limitation, we propose PredGAMM, a geometry-aware multimodal learning framework for the prediction of secreted effectors in Gram-negative bacteria. The key idea behind PredGAMM is to reformulate effector prediction from the perspective of joint sequence-structure representation.Specifically, PredGAMM first applies an attention mechanism to the embedding vectors generated by the pretrained protein language model ESM-2, so as to further extract contextual semantic information from protein sequences. It then employs a geometric vector perceptron (GVP) to encode local geometric patterns and residue-level spatial relationships from protein structures, enabling the joint modeling of semantic and conformational features. Building on this, PredGAMM further integrates an auxiliary contrastive learning objective with a tokenization-based multi-head attention mechanism to enhance cross-modal alignment between sequence and structure and to model fine-grained multimodal interactions, thereby improving the identification of key effector-related patterns. Experimental results show that PredGAMM achieves strong performance across multiple effector prediction tasks, maintaining low false-positive rates and high specificity, with particularly good performance in T4SE and T6SE identification. Ablation studies further showed that, even On datasets containing remote homologs, PredGAMM achieved better performance than models based solely on sequence features, indicating that structural information and cross-modal collaborative modeling play important roles in improving prediction performance. Genome-scale analysis also indicates that PredGAMM can not only accurately recover manually validated effectors, but also efficiently identify novel candidate proteins associated with secretion systems, providing strong support for effector repertoire expansion and functional exploration.
